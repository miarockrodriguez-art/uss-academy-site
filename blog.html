How to Get Your Concealed Weapons Permit (CWP) in Florida — Complete 2026 Guide

Getting your concealed weapons permit in Florida is one of the most important steps you can take for personal protection. Whether you're a first-time gun owner in Orlando or a lifelong Floridian who's been putting it off — this guide walks you through everything you need to know about the Florida concealed carry permit process in 2026.

What Is a Florida CWP?

A Florida Concealed Weapons Permit (CWP) — also called a CCW (Concealed Carry Weapon) license — allows you to legally carry a concealed firearm in the state of Florida and in 37+ states with reciprocity agreements. It's issued by the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS).

Requirements for a Florida Concealed Weapons Permit

To qualify for a concealed weapons permit in Florida, you must:

  • Be at least 21 years old (or 18+ for active military/veterans)
  • Be a U.S. citizen or legal permanent resident
  • Not have a felony conviction (unless civil rights have been restored)
  • Not be a habitual drug or alcohol user
  • Not have been adjudicated mentally incompetent
  • Not have a domestic violence conviction
  • Complete an approved firearms training course — this is where USS Academy comes in

What to Expect at a CWP Class in Orlando

A CWP class in Orlando through USS Academy typically includes:

  1. Classroom instruction — Florida firearm law (Statutes 790 and 776), use of force, self-defense law, safe handling and storage
  2. Live-fire qualification — Range time demonstrating safe firearm handling and basic marksmanship
  3. Certificate issuance — You receive a training certificate to submit with your FDACS application

The entire process takes about 4 hours. USS Academy classes are held at partner ranges across Central Florida — not at our corporate office. Contact us to confirm your class location before attending.

How to Apply After Your Class

  1. Complete your firearms training course and receive your certificate
  2. Submit your application online through the FDACS website or at a regional office
  3. Provide fingerprints (electronic submission available)
  4. Submit a passport-style photo
  5. Pay the application fee ($97 for initial, $45 for renewal)
  6. Wait for processing — typically 50-90 days

How Much Does a CWP Cost in Florida?

The total cost breaks down as:

  • Training course: $100–$200 depending on the provider (USS Academy classes start at $150)
  • FDACS application fee: $97 (initial) or $45 (renewal)
  • Fingerprinting: Typically included in the application fee

Florida Open Carry Law 2026 — What You Need to Know

Florida's firearms landscape has changed. After years as a concealed-carry-only state, open carry in Florida is now legal under specific conditions. Whether you currently hold a Florida concealed weapons permit or you're considering getting one — understanding the new open carry Florida laws is essential for every responsible gun owner.

What Changed?

Florida now allows the open carry of firearms for individuals who hold a valid concealed weapons permit (CWP). This means if you already have your Florida CWP, you can legally carry your firearm openly — visible and not concealed — in most public places where firearms are not otherwise prohibited.

Key Points About Florida Open Carry

  • You still need a CWP. Open carry without a valid concealed weapons permit remains illegal in Florida. The permit is the gateway to both concealed and open carry.
  • Prohibited locations still apply. Schools, government buildings, courthouses, bars, airports (past security), polling places, and other restricted zones remain off-limits — just like with concealed carry.
  • Private property rights remain. Business owners can prohibit firearms on their premises. If asked to leave, you must comply.
  • Law enforcement interactions. If approached by law enforcement while open carrying, remain calm, keep hands visible, and inform the officer that you hold a valid CWP. Carry your permit at all times.
  • Brandishing vs. carrying. There is a legal difference between lawfully carrying a firearm openly and brandishing or displaying a firearm in a threatening manner. Know the line.

Should You Open Carry?

Just because you can doesn't always mean you should. There are tactical, social, and practical considerations:

  • Tactical disadvantage: Open carry reveals that you're armed. In a threat situation, you may become the first target.
  • Public perception: Some people are uncomfortable around visible firearms. Be aware of your environment and audience.
  • Retention risk: An openly carried firearm is more accessible to someone attempting to take it from you. Invest in a quality retention holster.
  • Situational awareness: USS Academy recommends that open carry be a deliberate, informed decision — not a default. Understand when open carry is and is not advisable.

First-Time Gun Owner? 5 Firearm Safety Rules Every Florida Resident Should Know

Congratulations on making the decision to exercise your Second Amendment rights. Whether you purchased your first firearm for home defense, personal protection, or sport — gun safety in Florida starts the moment that firearm enters your life. At USS Academy, we've trained over 29,000 students and we can tell you from experience: the fundamentals never get old.

Here are the 5 firearm safety rules every Florida gun owner — new or experienced — should know by heart.

1. Treat Every Firearm as If It's Loaded

This is rule number one for a reason. Every time you pick up a firearm — whether you just watched someone clear it, whether it's been sitting in your safe for months, whether you're absolutely certain it's empty — treat it as if it's loaded. This mindset eliminates complacency, which is the number one cause of negligent discharges.

At USS Academy's firearms training in Orlando, this is the first thing every student learns, and the last thing we reinforce before they leave.

2. Never Point a Firearm at Anything You're Not Willing to Destroy

Muzzle awareness — knowing where your gun is pointed at all times — is a non-negotiable skill. In our training classes, we call this "muzzle discipline." Whether you're at the range, cleaning your firearm at home, or carrying in public — always be conscious of where the muzzle is pointing.

Bullets don't come back. If it's not a target you intend to engage, don't let the muzzle cross it. Period.

3. Keep Your Finger Off the Trigger Until Ready to Fire

Your trigger finger should rest along the frame of the firearm — not inside the trigger guard — until you have made the conscious decision to fire. This applies at the range, during dry-fire practice, and especially in high-stress situations.

We see this mistake more than almost any other with new shooters. It's a habit that takes deliberate training to build — and one session at USS Academy will hardwire it into your muscle memory.

4. Know Your Target and What's Beyond It

Bullets travel through walls, doors, car panels, and soft barriers. Before you fire — whether at a range or in a defensive situation — you need to know what's behind and around your target. In firearms training, this is called "target identification and backstop awareness."

In a home defense situation, this is critical. Knowing the layout of your home, where family members sleep, and what walls are between you and your target can mean the difference between protecting your family and putting them at risk.

5. Store Firearms Securely and Responsibly

Florida law requires that if you live with minors (under 18), firearms must be stored securely or with a trigger lock mechanism. But responsible storage goes beyond legal requirements:

  • Use a gun safe or lockbox for all firearms not in your immediate control
  • Store ammunition separately from firearms when possible
  • Teach every member of your household basic firearm safety — even if they never intend to shoot
  • Consider quick-access safes for home defense firearms — balancing security with accessibility

Florida-specific note: Under Florida Statute 790.174, if a minor gains access to a negligently stored firearm and uses it, the firearm owner can face criminal charges. Secure storage isn't just smart — it's the law.

What's Next?

If you're a first-time gun owner in Florida, the best investment you can make is professional firearms training. Reading about safety is a great start — but there's no substitute for hands-on instruction from certified professionals who can correct your grip, your stance, and your habits in real time.

USS Academy offers beginner-friendly classes every week across Central Florida. You don't need to own a firearm to attend — and you don't need any experience. We'll meet you exactly where you are.

Florida Statute 790 Explained — What Every Florida Gun Owner Needs to Know

Florida Statute 790 is the chapter of Florida law that governs weapons and firearms. If you own a gun in Florida, carry concealed, or are considering getting your CWP — this is the law that defines what you can and cannot do. At USS Academy, we teach Statute 790 in every CWP class and advanced law course because understanding this statute is not optional. It is the foundation of legal firearm ownership in this state.

What Does Florida Statute 790 Cover?

Chapter 790 of the Florida Statutes is titled "Weapons and Firearms" and it spans dozens of individual sections. The key areas every gun owner should understand include:

  • 790.01 — Unlicensed carrying of concealed weapons. This section defines the crime of carrying a concealed weapon without a license and lays out the penalties — a first-degree misdemeanor for non-firearms, a third-degree felony for firearms.
  • 790.06 — License to carry concealed weapon or firearm. This is the CWP section. It details eligibility, the application process, training requirements, and reciprocity.
  • 790.053 — Open carrying of weapons. Updated to reflect Florida's new open carry provisions for CWP holders.
  • 790.10 — Improper exhibition of dangerous weapons or firearms. Displaying a firearm in a rude, careless, angry, or threatening manner is a first-degree misdemeanor — separate from lawful open carry.
  • 790.15 — Discharging firearms in public. Firing a gun in a public place or over a road is a first-degree misdemeanor, with enhanced penalties if property damage or injury results.
  • 790.174 — Safe storage of firearms. If you live with minors, Florida law requires firearms to be stored securely. Violation can result in criminal charges if a minor accesses the weapon.
  • 790.25 — Lawful ownership, possession, and use of firearms. This section affirms the right to keep and bear arms and lists lawful exemptions for possession, transport, and use.
  • 790.33 — Preemption of local firearms regulations. Only the state legislature can regulate firearms in Florida — local governments cannot pass their own gun ordinances.

Common Misunderstandings About 790

Many Florida gun owners unknowingly operate under incorrect assumptions. Here are the most frequent misunderstandings we address in USS Academy firearms training:

  • "I can carry anywhere with my CWP." False. Section 790.06(12) lists specific prohibited locations including schools, government buildings, courthouses, polling places, bars, and airports past security.
  • "Printing means I'm breaking the law." Briefly printing — where the outline of a concealed weapon is momentarily visible — is generally not considered a violation. However, deliberate display can cross into improper exhibition under 790.10.
  • "My car is an extension of my home." Florida law does allow securely encased firearms in a vehicle without a CWP (790.25), but "securely encased" has a specific legal definition — a glove compartment, snapped holster, gun case, or closed box.

Why This Matters for Your Training

Understanding Florida Statute 790 is not about memorizing legal codes. It is about making informed decisions under pressure. When you know the law, you carry with confidence. When you guess, you risk a felony. Florida Statute 776 — Stand Your Ground and Justifiable Use of Force Explained

If you carry a firearm in Florida — or even keep one in your home for protection — Florida Statute 776 is the law that determines whether your use of force is justified or criminal. This is the chapter that contains Florida's well-known Stand Your Ground law, the Castle Doctrine, and the legal framework for justifiable use of deadly force. At USS Academy, we consider this the single most important body of law for every armed citizen to understand.

What Is Florida's Stand Your Ground Law?

Under Section 776.012, a person who is in a place where they have a right to be has no duty to retreat before using or threatening to use force — including deadly force — if they reasonably believe it is necessary to prevent imminent death, great bodily harm, or the commission of a forcible felony. This is the core of Stand Your Ground. Unlike states that impose a duty to retreat, Florida law allows you to hold your position and defend yourself.

Key Sections of Statute 776

  • 776.012 — Use or threatened use of force in defense of person. Permits non-deadly force when you reasonably believe it is necessary to defend against imminent unlawful force. Permits deadly force when you reasonably believe it is necessary to prevent imminent death, great bodily harm, or a forcible felony.
  • 776.013 — Home protection (Castle Doctrine). Creates a legal presumption that you had reasonable fear if an intruder unlawfully and forcefully entered your dwelling, residence, or occupied vehicle. This is a powerful legal protection for homeowners and vehicle occupants.
  • 776.031 — Use of force in defense of others. You may use force — including deadly force — to protect another person if you reasonably believe they face imminent death or great bodily harm.
  • 776.032 — Immunity from prosecution. A person who uses justified force under this statute is immune from criminal prosecution and civil action. This immunity can be raised in a pre-trial hearing where the burden shifts to the prosecution to prove by clear and convincing evidence that the use of force was not justified.
  • 776.041 — Use of force by aggressors. If you are the initial aggressor, you generally lose the right to claim self-defense — unless you withdraw from the encounter and clearly communicate your withdrawal, and the other person continues or escalates the use of force.

What "Reasonable Belief" Means

The most critical phrase in Statute 776 is "reasonably believes." This is an objective standard — meaning a jury will evaluate whether a reasonable person in your situation, knowing what you knew at the time, would have believed that deadly force was necessary. It is not enough that you felt afraid. The fear must be one that a rational person would share under the same circumstances. This is why training matters. USS Academy's defensive training courses put you through scenario-based exercises that build the judgment skills necessary to make these split-second decisions correctly.

Common Mistakes That Destroy a Self-Defense Claim

  • Pursuing a retreating attacker. Once the threat stops, your legal justification stops. Following someone who is running away can turn you into the aggressor.
  • Verbal provocation without physical threat. Words alone — no matter how threatening — do not justify deadly force in Florida.
  • Using deadly force to protect property alone. Florida does not permit lethal force solely to protect property. There must be a reasonable fear of death or great bodily harm.
  • Failing to call 911 immediately. The first person to call law enforcement is often perceived as the victim. Delay can be interpreted as consciousness of guilt.

Florida Open Carry Law 2026 — Complete Guide for Florida Gun Owners

Florida's open carry law has changed the way firearms are carried in the Sunshine State. For decades, Florida was strictly a concealed-carry state. Now, with updated legislation, CWP holders can legally carry their firearms openly in most public spaces. But "legal" does not mean "simple." This complete guide from USS Academy — Florida's largest firearms training academy with 29,000+ graduates — breaks down everything you need to know about open carry in Florida in 2026.

The Legal Foundation: What Changed

Florida now permits open carry for individuals who hold a valid Florida concealed weapons permit (CWP). This means the same permit that allows you to carry concealed now also authorizes you to carry openly — with your firearm visible in a holster or on your person. Without a CWP, open carry remains illegal and can result in arrest and criminal charges.

Where You Can and Cannot Open Carry

The prohibited locations for open carry mirror those for concealed carry under Florida Statute 790.06(12):

  • Schools and school events — all K-12 campuses and sponsored activities
  • Colleges and universities — including campus grounds and buildings
  • Government buildings — courthouses, legislative chambers, city halls
  • Polling places — during election periods
  • Bars and establishments — where the primary revenue comes from alcohol sales
  • Airports — past the security checkpoint
  • Professional sports events — at venues with specific signage
  • Private property — any property owner can prohibit firearms on their premises

Tactical Considerations from USS Academy Instructors

Our Law Enforcement Firearms Instructors and USMC-trained staff offer this practical guidance for anyone considering open carry:

  • Invest in a Level II or Level III retention holster. An openly carried firearm is visible and accessible to others. A quality retention holster prevents unauthorized access.
  • Be prepared for public interaction. Not everyone is comfortable seeing firearms in public. You may be approached, questioned, or asked to leave private property.
  • Know the difference between open carry and brandishing. Lawfully carrying a holstered firearm is legal. Touching, adjusting, or displaying your firearm in a manner that could be perceived as threatening can constitute improper exhibition under Statute 790.10.
  • Consider the tactical trade-off. Concealed carry maintains the element of surprise. Open carry announces your capability. In a defensive scenario, an openly carried firearm may make you the primary target.

Law Enforcement Interactions While Open Carrying

If approached by law enforcement while openly carrying in Florida:

  1. Keep your hands visible at all times — do not reach for your firearm
  2. Calmly inform the officer that you have a valid Florida CWP
  3. Provide your CWP and government ID when requested
  4. Follow all instructions — you can address any disagreements later through proper legal channels

USS Academy tip: Whether you choose to carry concealed or open, the legal and tactical knowledge you need is the same. Our CWP and advanced carry classes cover both modalities in depth — because how you carry should be a trained, deliberate decision.

How to Choose Your First Handgun — USS Academy Complete Guide

Buying your first handgun is a significant decision — and one that too many people make based on a friend's recommendation, a movie scene, or whatever is on sale at the gun shop. At USS Academy, we have helped over 29,000 students make informed firearms decisions over 27+ years, and we can tell you this: the right first handgun is the one that fits your hand, your purpose, and your training level. Here is how to choose wisely.

Define Your Purpose First

Before you look at a single firearm, answer this question: what is this gun for?

  • Home defense — You want reliability, capacity, and a size you can operate under stress. Full-size handguns with good sights and manageable recoil are ideal.
  • Concealed carry — Size and weight matter. You need something compact enough to carry daily without printing, but large enough to shoot accurately under pressure.
  • Range and sport shooting — Comfort, accuracy, and trigger quality are priorities. Full-size handguns in 9mm are the standard starting point.
  • General personal protection — A mid-size (compact) handgun in 9mm covers the most ground for most people.

Caliber: Start With 9mm

For the vast majority of first-time handgun buyers, 9mm Luger is the right answer. It offers manageable recoil, affordable ammunition, high magazine capacity, and — with modern defensive ammunition — terminal performance that rivals larger calibers. Law enforcement agencies, military units, and professional trainers worldwide have standardized on 9mm for good reason. Our firearms training classes use 9mm extensively for exactly these reasons.

Semi-Auto vs. Revolver

The debate is mostly settled for defensive use. Modern semi-automatic pistols offer higher capacity (typically 10-17+ rounds), faster reloads, and slimmer profiles for concealment. Revolvers are simple and reliable but limited to 5-6 rounds with slow reloads. For a first handgun in 2026, a quality semi-automatic in 9mm is the standard recommendation from our instructors.

Fit and Ergonomics

A handgun must fit your hand. This is non-negotiable. When you grip the firearm:

  • The web of your hand should sit high on the backstrap with no gaps
  • Your trigger finger should reach the trigger face comfortably at the first pad of the fingertip
  • All controls — slide stop, magazine release, safety if applicable — should be reachable without shifting your grip
  • The overall weight and balance should feel natural, not front-heavy or awkward

Visit a range that rents handguns — or attend a USS Academy training class where you can handle and fire different models under instructor guidance.

Brands and Models to Consider

For first-time buyers, proven reliability is everything. The firearms industry's most trusted first-handgun options include the Glock 19 (the standard compact 9mm), Smith & Wesson M&P 2.0 Compact, Sig Sauer P320, Springfield Hellcat or XD series, and the Ruger Security-9. All are available at Florida gun shops and all perform well in our training courses.

Budget Expectations

A quality first handgun costs between $400 and $650 new. Add $50-$100 for a good holster, $30-$50 for a cleaning kit, and budget for ammunition — at least 200 rounds for initial training. Do not buy the cheapest option available. A reliable firearm is not the place to save money.

Best Firearms for Home Defense in Florida

Choosing the right firearm for home defense is one of the most consequential decisions a Florida homeowner can make. The stakes are high — you are selecting a tool that may one day stand between your family and a violent threat. At USS Academy, our instructors — who hold Law Enforcement Firearms, USMC, NRA, USCCA, and Florida K License certifications — have helped thousands of Florida residents make this decision with clarity. Here is what we recommend.

Understanding Home Defense Requirements

A home defense firearm has different demands than a range gun or a concealed carry weapon. In a home defense scenario, you need:

  • Reliability above all else. The firearm must fire every single time you pull the trigger. No exceptions.
  • Manageable recoil. Under the stress of a home invasion, fine motor skills degrade. You need a firearm that every adult in the home can operate.
  • Adequate capacity. Multiple threats or missed shots under stress mean you need enough rounds to resolve the situation.
  • Over-penetration awareness. Florida homes often have drywall construction. Rounds that pass through walls can endanger family members in adjacent rooms.
  • Weapon-mounted light capability. Home invasions overwhelmingly occur at night. Target identification in the dark is not optional — it is a legal requirement under Florida Statute 776.

Top Home Defense Firearms Categories

Handguns for Home Defense

A full-size 9mm handgun is the most versatile home defense option. Models like the Glock 17, Smith & Wesson M&P 2.0 Full Size, or Sig Sauer P320 offer 17+ round capacity, proven reliability, and accessory rail for a weapon light. Pair with quality defensive ammunition (Federal HST, Speer Gold Dot, or Hornady Critical Duty) for optimal terminal performance with reduced wall penetration compared to rifle calibers.

Shotguns for Home Defense

The 12-gauge shotgun remains a devastating home defense option. The Mossberg 590 and Remington 870 are proven platforms. Loaded with 00 buckshot, a shotgun delivers immediate stopping power. However, recoil is significant, capacity is limited (typically 6-8 rounds), and reloading under stress is slow. Consider a 20-gauge for family members with smaller frames. Our defensive firearms classes include shotgun training modules.

Pistol-Caliber Carbines and AR-Platform Rifles

Night Vision Training — Is It Worth It?

Night vision and low-light firearms training has moved from the exclusive domain of special operations and law enforcement into the civilian training world. The equipment is more accessible, the training opportunities are expanding, and the reality that most violent encounters occur in low-light conditions makes this a relevant question for serious shooters. At USS Academy, we offer advanced training programs that include low-light and night operations — and here is our honest assessment of whether night vision training is worth your time and investment.

The Case for Night Vision Training

The data supports it: the FBI reports that approximately 70% of officer-involved shootings occur in low-light or no-light conditions. Civilian defensive encounters follow similar patterns — home invasions, parking lot robberies, and street crimes overwhelmingly happen after dark. If your entire training history involves well-lit indoor ranges during business hours, you are training for the minority of real-world scenarios.

What Night Vision Training Covers

A quality night vision and low-light training course typically includes:

  • Weapon-mounted light techniques. How to use a pistol or rifle-mounted light for target identification, search patterns, and threat assessment.
  • Handheld flashlight techniques. The Harries, FBI, Syringe, and other established techniques for pairing a handheld light with a handgun.
  • Night vision goggle (NVG) operation. For courses that include NVGs — how to move, aim, and fire under NODs (Night Observation Devices), including the use of IR lasers and illuminators.
  • Transition drills. Moving between lighted and dark environments, adjusting to changing light conditions, and maintaining accuracy throughout.
  • Target identification under stress. Positive identification of threats in the dark is both a legal requirement and a moral imperative. This training builds that skill.

Who Should Consider Night Vision Training?

  • Home defense-oriented shooters. If your primary concern is protecting your family in your home, low-light training is directly applicable. Home invasions happen at night.
  • CWP holders who carry daily. If you carry a concealed weapon, you carry it in the dark, too. Can you operate your weapon light? Can you identify a threat in a poorly lit parking garage?
  • Competitive shooters. Low-light stages are increasingly common in practical shooting competitions.
  • Law enforcement and security professionals. For obvious reasons — and USS Academy works extensively with LE professionals.

The Cost Reality

Night vision equipment is expensive. Quality NVGs (PVS-14 or dual-tube units) range from $2,500 to $10,000+. IR lasers and illuminators add $500-$2,000. However, the training itself does not require you to own NVGs — many courses provide loaner equipment. The more immediately practical investment is a quality weapon-mounted light ($100-$300) and a course focused on low-light handgun techniques, which translates directly to home defense and daily carry scenarios.

Our Honest Assessment

For most civilian shooters, weapon-mounted light and handheld flashlight training is absolutely worth it and should be considered essential — not optional. Full night vision goggle training is a valuable but specialized skill set best pursued after you have mastered fundamentals, defensive shooting, and low-light techniques. Start with the basics, then advance. USS Academy's training progression is designed to take you through this path in the right order.

Bottom line: Can you identify a target in your dark hallway at 3 AM? Can you operate your weapon light while managing recoil? If the answer is no, low-light training is your next step. USS Academy offers courses that build these skills progressively — from basic flashlight techniques to advanced night operations.

How to Clean and Maintain Your Firearm — Complete Guide

A firearm is a precision mechanical tool, and like any tool, it requires regular maintenance to function reliably. At USS Academy, we have seen the consequences of neglected firearms — malfunctions at the range, failures during qualification, and worst of all, weapons that cannot be trusted when lives depend on them. Across 27+ years and 29,000+ students, we teach every graduate that firearm maintenance is not optional. Here is the complete guide.

Why Cleaning Your Firearm Matters

Every time you fire a round, combustion residue — carbon, copper, lead, and unburned powder — deposits inside the barrel, chamber, and action. Over time, this fouling degrades accuracy, causes malfunctions (failure to feed, failure to eject, failure to fire), and accelerates wear. Florida's high humidity adds another threat: corrosion. Salt air, sweat from concealed carry, and ambient moisture can rust steel components in days if left unprotected.

Essential Cleaning Supplies

  • Cleaning rod or bore snake — sized for your firearm's caliber
  • Bore brush — bronze bristle for scrubbing fouling from the barrel
  • Cleaning patches — cotton patches for the bore and chamber
  • Solvent — Hoppe's No. 9, Break-Free CLP, or Ballistol are proven choices
  • Lubricant — A quality gun oil or CLP (cleaner-lubricant-protectant)
  • Nylon brush — For scrubbing external components without scratching
  • Microfiber cloth — For wiping down exterior surfaces
  • Cleaning mat — Protects your work surface and keeps small parts organized

Step-by-Step Cleaning Process

  1. Safety first. Remove the magazine. Lock the slide back. Visually and physically verify the chamber is empty. Point the firearm in a safe direction throughout the entire process.
  2. Field strip the firearm. Consult your owner's manual for your specific model. For most semi-automatic pistols, this means removing the slide, barrel, recoil spring, and guide rod. Do not detail strip unless you are trained to do so.
  3. Clean the barrel. Run a solvent-soaked bore brush through the barrel 10-15 passes. Follow with solvent-soaked patches until they come through clean. Finish with a lightly oiled patch for protection.
  4. Clean the slide and frame. Use solvent and a nylon brush to scrub carbon buildup from the slide rails, breech face, extractor, and firing pin channel. Wipe down the frame rails and trigger assembly.
  5. Lubricate. Apply a thin film of gun oil to the barrel exterior, slide rails, barrel hood, and any other metal-on-metal contact points specified in your manual. Less is more — excess oil attracts dirt and can impede function.
  6. Reassemble and function check. Reassemble the firearm per the manual. Perform a function check — verify the slide locks back on an empty magazine, the trigger resets properly, and all controls operate normally.
  7. Wipe down the exterior. Use a lightly oiled cloth to wipe down all external metal surfaces. This prevents fingerprint corrosion — especially important in Florida's humid climate.

Maintenance Schedule

  • After every range session: Full cleaning and lubrication
  • Every 2 weeks (carry guns): Wipe down, check for lint and debris, re-lubricate
  • Every 3-6 months (stored firearms): Full cleaning, rust inspection, fresh lubrication
  • Annually: Detail strip or professional armorer inspection for carry and home defense firearms

USS Academy teaches maintenance: Firearm cleaning and maintenance is covered in our beginner and intermediate training classes. Learning to field strip, clean, and reassemble your firearm under instructor supervision builds confidence and ensures you are doing it right from day one.

Family Firearms Safety — Teaching Your Children About Gun Safety

If you own a firearm and have children in your home, family firearms safety is not optional — it is an absolute responsibility. At USS Academy, we have trained over 29,000 students across 27+ years, and a significant portion of our graduates are parents who came to us asking the same question: how do I keep my family safe while also keeping a firearm accessible for protection? The answer is a combination of secure storage, age-appropriate education, and a household culture built on safety and respect.

The Florida Legal Requirement

Before we discuss education, let's address the law. Florida Statute 790.174 states that a person who stores or leaves a loaded firearm within the reach or easy access of a minor (under 16 years old) commits a misdemeanor of the second degree — and if the minor obtains the firearm and uses it to injure or kill someone, the firearm owner faces a felony charge. This is not theoretical. It is enforced. Secure storage is the first and most critical element of family firearms safety in Florida.

Secure Storage That Works

The challenge for parents who keep firearms for home defense is balancing quick access with child-proof security. Here are the proven solutions:

  • Biometric quick-access safes — Fingerprint-activated safes mounted to a nightstand or inside a closet. Access in under 2 seconds for authorized users, impossible for children.
  • Mechanical push-button safes — Simplex-style locks that require a specific button combination. No batteries, no biometrics — just a combination you memorize.
  • Full-size gun safes — For long guns and firearms not used for immediate home defense. Bolted to the floor or wall.
  • Trigger locks and cable locks — Supplementary security for firearms in transit or as an additional layer inside a safe.

Age-Appropriate Firearms Education

The NRA's Eddie Eagle program — which USS Academy supports — teaches children four simple rules: Stop. Don't touch. Run away. Tell an adult. This is the baseline for children under age 8. But education should evolve with your child's maturity:

Ages 4-7: Awareness and Avoidance

At this age, the message is simple and absolute: firearms are not toys. If you see one, do not touch it. Leave the area immediately and tell an adult. Reinforce this message regularly and without exceptions. Do not rely on a single conversation — children need repetition.

Ages 8-12: Supervised Introduction

If you choose to introduce your child to firearms at this stage, it should be structured, supervised, and safety-focused. Start with the four universal safety rules. Use an unloaded firearm for handling familiarization. Progress to supervised shooting with a .22 LR rifle at a controlled range — never in an informal setting. USS Academy offers youth-friendly training options for this age group with parental participation required.

Ages 13-17: Skill Building and Responsibility

Teenagers who have been properly introduced to firearms can begin developing real skill. This includes marksmanship fundamentals, cleaning and maintenance, and an age-appropriate understanding of Florida firearms law. At this stage, the conversation shifts from "don't touch" to "this is a serious responsibility." Many USS Academy families enroll teenagers in our beginner courses alongside a parent — learning together builds a shared safety culture.

The Conversation Every Parent Must Have

Regardless of whether you choose to teach your children to shoot, you must have the safety conversation. Children encounter firearms at friends' houses, in public, and in media. The question is not whether they will be exposed — it is whether they will know what to do. Key topics include:

  • What to do if they find a firearm anywhere — at home, at a friend's house, or in public
  • The difference between real firearms and toys/movies/video games
  • That they will never be in trouble for telling you about a firearm they encountered
  • That peer pressure to handle or show off a firearm is dangerous and must be refused

Leading by Example

Children learn more from what you do than what you say. If they see you handling firearms safely, cleaning them routinely, storing them securely, and treating them with respect — they absorb that culture. If they see carelessness, they learn that too.
