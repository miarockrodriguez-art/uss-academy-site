How to Get Your Concealed Weapons Permit (CWP) in Florida — Complete 2026 Guide

Getting your concealed weapons permit in Florida is one of the most important steps you can take for personal protection. Whether you're a first-time gun owner in Orlando or a lifelong Floridian who's been putting it off — this guide walks you through everything you need to know about the Florida concealed carry permit process in 2026.

What Is a Florida CWP?

A Florida Concealed Weapons Permit (CWP) — also called a CCW (Concealed Carry Weapon) license — allows you to legally carry a concealed firearm in the state of Florida and in 37+ states with reciprocity agreements. It's issued by the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS).

Requirements for a Florida Concealed Weapons Permit

To qualify for a concealed weapons permit in Florida, you must:

  • Be at least 21 years old (or 18+ for active military/veterans)
  • Be a U.S. citizen or legal permanent resident
  • Not have a felony conviction (unless civil rights have been restored)
  • Not be a habitual drug or alcohol user
  • Not have been adjudicated mentally incompetent
  • Not have a domestic violence conviction
  • Complete an approved firearms training course — this is where USS Academy comes in

What to Expect at a CWP Class in Orlando

A CWP class in Orlando through USS Academy typically includes:

  1. Classroom instruction — Florida firearm law (Statutes 790 and 776), use of force, self-defense law, safe handling and storage
  2. Live-fire qualification — Range time demonstrating safe firearm handling and basic marksmanship
  3. Certificate issuance — You receive a training certificate to submit with your FDACS application

The entire process takes about 4 hours. USS Academy classes are held at partner ranges across Central Florida — not at our corporate office. Contact us to confirm your class location before attending.

How to Apply After Your Class

  1. Complete your firearms training course and receive your certificate
  2. Submit your application online through the FDACS website or at a regional office
  3. Provide fingerprints (electronic submission available)
  4. Submit a passport-style photo
  5. Pay the application fee ($97 for initial, $45 for renewal)
  6. Wait for processing — typically 50-90 days

How Much Does a CWP Cost in Florida?

The total cost breaks down as:

  • Training course: $100–$200 depending on the provider (USS Academy classes start at $150)
  • FDACS application fee: $97 (initial) or $45 (renewal)
  • Fingerprinting: Typically included in the application fee

Florida Open Carry Law 2026 — What You Need to Know

Florida's firearms landscape has changed. After years as a concealed-carry-only state, open carry in Florida is now legal under specific conditions. Whether you currently hold a Florida concealed weapons permit or you're considering getting one — understanding the new open carry Florida laws is essential for every responsible gun owner.

What Changed?

Florida now allows the open carry of firearms for individuals who hold a valid concealed weapons permit (CWP). This means if you already have your Florida CWP, you can legally carry your firearm openly — visible and not concealed — in most public places where firearms are not otherwise prohibited.

Key Points About Florida Open Carry

  • You still need a CWP. Open carry without a valid concealed weapons permit remains illegal in Florida. The permit is the gateway to both concealed and open carry.
  • Prohibited locations still apply. Schools, government buildings, courthouses, bars, airports (past security), polling places, and other restricted zones remain off-limits — just like with concealed carry.
  • Private property rights remain. Business owners can prohibit firearms on their premises. If asked to leave, you must comply.
  • Law enforcement interactions. If approached by law enforcement while open carrying, remain calm, keep hands visible, and inform the officer that you hold a valid CWP. Carry your permit at all times.
  • Brandishing vs. carrying. There is a legal difference between lawfully carrying a firearm openly and brandishing or displaying a firearm in a threatening manner. Know the line.

Should You Open Carry?

Just because you can doesn't always mean you should. There are tactical, social, and practical considerations:

  • Tactical disadvantage: Open carry reveals that you're armed. In a threat situation, you may become the first target.
  • Public perception: Some people are uncomfortable around visible firearms. Be aware of your environment and audience.
  • Retention risk: An openly carried firearm is more accessible to someone attempting to take it from you. Invest in a quality retention holster.
  • Situational awareness: USS Academy recommends that open carry be a deliberate, informed decision — not a default. Understand when open carry is and is not advisable.

First-Time Gun Owner? 5 Firearm Safety Rules Every Florida Resident Should Know

Congratulations on making the decision to exercise your Second Amendment rights. Whether you purchased your first firearm for home defense, personal protection, or sport — gun safety in Florida starts the moment that firearm enters your life. At USS Academy, we've trained over 29,000 students and we can tell you from experience: the fundamentals never get old.

Here are the 5 firearm safety rules every Florida gun owner — new or experienced — should know by heart.

1. Treat Every Firearm as If It's Loaded

This is rule number one for a reason. Every time you pick up a firearm — whether you just watched someone clear it, whether it's been sitting in your safe for months, whether you're absolutely certain it's empty — treat it as if it's loaded. This mindset eliminates complacency, which is the number one cause of negligent discharges.

At USS Academy's firearms training in Orlando, this is the first thing every student learns, and the last thing we reinforce before they leave.

2. Never Point a Firearm at Anything You're Not Willing to Destroy

Muzzle awareness — knowing where your gun is pointed at all times — is a non-negotiable skill. In our training classes, we call this "muzzle discipline." Whether you're at the range, cleaning your firearm at home, or carrying in public — always be conscious of where the muzzle is pointing.

Bullets don't come back. If it's not a target you intend to engage, don't let the muzzle cross it. Period.

3. Keep Your Finger Off the Trigger Until Ready to Fire

Your trigger finger should rest along the frame of the firearm — not inside the trigger guard — until you have made the conscious decision to fire. This applies at the range, during dry-fire practice, and especially in high-stress situations.

We see this mistake more than almost any other with new shooters. It's a habit that takes deliberate training to build — and one session at USS Academy will hardwire it into your muscle memory.

4. Know Your Target and What's Beyond It

Bullets travel through walls, doors, car panels, and soft barriers. Before you fire — whether at a range or in a defensive situation — you need to know what's behind and around your target. In firearms training, this is called "target identification and backstop awareness."

In a home defense situation, this is critical. Knowing the layout of your home, where family members sleep, and what walls are between you and your target can mean the difference between protecting your family and putting them at risk.

5. Store Firearms Securely and Responsibly

Florida law requires that if you live with minors (under 18), firearms must be stored securely or with a trigger lock mechanism. But responsible storage goes beyond legal requirements:

  • Use a gun safe or lockbox for all firearms not in your immediate control
  • Store ammunition separately from firearms when possible
  • Teach every member of your household basic firearm safety — even if they never intend to shoot
  • Consider quick-access safes for home defense firearms — balancing security with accessibility

Florida-specific note: Under Florida Statute 790.174, if a minor gains access to a negligently stored firearm and uses it, the firearm owner can face criminal charges. Secure storage isn't just smart — it's the law.
